I still have not booked or planned my return flight. As much as there are parts of me that want to go home, there are parts of me that do not want to go back. What is waiting for me back at home? Like I explained in my video or at least try to articulate, I thought coming abroad would be a clean break. Changing my coordinates would automatically change my head. And it did, I got to experience a different rhythm of life. But for the most part, depression did not stay behind, it got on the flight with me. As the end of the semester approaches, I am scared. Scared of throwing myself back in the rat race that broke my baselines in the first place. The endless optimization, the relentless pressure I put on myself, and the hyper-competition all feelings I know too well.

When I talk to Japanese students or other international students, they are always intrigued by the American Dream. Almost intoxicated by it. When you look at it from their perspective, one can understand how the construct of having drive, raw ambition, and the promise that if you outwork everyone else, you win is so enticing. I now know what Anthony Bourdain meant when he said, "No one understands and appreciates the American Dream of hard work leading to material rewards better than a non-American." It is beautiful. 

But being an American, I also see the shadow side. I know what happens when dreams turn into an obsession with checking boxes. This is where Tyler Durden took a hold of me and gave me a revelation to the meaning of line: "Self-improvement is masturbation."

We or I have been conditioned to think that if we just optimize enough, study enough, track our neural pathways enough, and buy into the perfect routine, we’ll magically fix our lives. We treat "self-improvement" like a consumable to make ourselves feel productive while we are all just frozen, hiding from the world. Bourdain illustrates how seductive the pursuit is, but Durden exposes the arrival fallacy.

So that is where I’m torn. I don't want to lose my drive, do the things I want to do, and reap the rewards of hard work. But I refuse to let the race hollow me out again, and I refuse to let 'self-improvement' become just another ouroboros of mental comfort that hinders me from living. And maybe that is the real shift. Bourdain also said: "Travel is not a reward for working, it's education for living."

Bourdain also committed suicide, so there is a huge disconnect to how much of his words I take into account. It is awfully sad how the most beautiful people are the most broken. Bourdain and Robin Williams are both people who gave the world so much joy and profound perspective, but could not survive the battle between their own ears.

It makes you realize that the exact same depth, sensitivity, and openness that allows someone to see the world so beautifully is the very thing that makes the internal weight of living so heavy to carry. When you turn the volume up on your ability to care, to notice, and to feel, you turn the volume up on the pain, too.

Their stories are a sobering reminder that insight alone cannot save you. You can not intellectualize your way out of a dark room or your own suffering. Just reading enough quotes or understanding enough philosophy will not fix your brain's chemistry. Even therapist need their own therapist.
