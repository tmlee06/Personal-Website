The first week of classes has officially wrapped, and we are now in Week 2/3. I say "Week 2/3" because the semester started April 8th on a Wednesday, and my classes do not meet two or three times a week, except my Japanese language class. I am taking the bare minimum 7 courses; Japanese language, Japanese Popular Culture, Basics of Informatics and AI, Fundamentals of Artificial Intelligence, Python Programming Practice, and Introduction to Behavioral Neuroscience. It's actually 6 courses, but my Japanese language course counts for two courses since lectures are twice a week. I am so happy I won the lottery for that course because my humanities general education requirement is completely wiped out. Kyoto University is giving me 8 credits as the course articulates to 8 hours of work a week when in reality, it is just four 90-minute lectures, totaling 6 hours. I guess the Japanese education system loves to round and are generous with how much credit they give. All my courses are a cakewalk, except my Japanese language course 😂. Most of my courses do not have a final exam nor a weekly assignment, so I should probably start studying for the upcoming batch of ECE courses I have to take this Fall back at UCSD. Kyoto University reminds me so much like community college in a sense that the lecture rooms are tiny and the student to faculty ratio is so small. The reason I say my courses are a cakewalk is because although they are meant for international exchange students, Kyoto University students can take them too, and it is more catered to them for improving their English. Although my English courses seem very easy to me, I really like the catering towards Japanese students' method of teaching because it helps breaks down these big concepts such as Artificial Intelligence and Neuroscience to the absolute bone as to where a 5 year-old can understand. I was sitting in my Python Programming Practice course the other day, listening to the lecture, and it just hit me as to why all my international colleagues at UCSD are so good at coding (particularly my Chinese colleagues, you know who you are). They have such strong coding skills because they have been performing a double-compilation in their heads since day one. Since C++, Java, and Python are all built on English-based syntax, they did not have the luxury of just 'skimming' the code and getting the gist of it like a native speaker might. They had to learn the logic, syntax, and the linguistics simultaneously. Not relying on intuition, but focusing on mastery of the actual mechanics. It is like that Japanese teaching style I am seeing now—by breaking everything down to the absolute bone because of the language barrier, they end up understanding the 'why' behind a line of code better than people who speak the language natively. By the time they get to a place like UCSD, that extra mental heavy-lifting has turned into a massive competitive advantage. I am going to talk to my neuroscience professor about this, but he might hit me with and say, "This is just basic neuroscience and how humans biologically learn." My neuroscience professor a cool guy, he is from Philly (short for Philadelphia), does brain research on monkeys with brain-computer-interfaces, and why eye movements weaken after head injuries. He offered me a tour of his lab and to listen in on some of his research meetings, so I am pretty stoked about that.
