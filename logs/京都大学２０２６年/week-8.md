If I could speak Japanese better, I would have chewed him out, but I just said, "すみませんでした、わかりました," which translates to "I am sorry, I understand." The respect to seniority and authority is at the max in Japan and I strongly disagree with it. I am not with the senpai or kohai hierarchy, I am just that guy. I thought that was a bar, it may as well be, but that tradition is more present than ever. Japanese people or my teammates just let this ice time and unethical practice time injustice happen to them without doing anything. I talk and complain too much about hockey when I am washed and retired, so let us move on from that.

When I read the title of the paper, <a href="https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/stamp/stamp.jsp?arnumber=11006954" style="color: #fffefe; font-weight: bold; text-decoration: underline;">Robot-Mediated Multi-Party Conversation Aimed at Affect Improvement for Psychiatric Patients</a>, it immediately triggered a Blade Runner 2049 or Her alarm in my head. Imitation is all an AI can do. After watching Blade Runner 2049 and Her, I do not wish to live in that reality, but in a sense we or I am living in it right now. Japan takes AI, robotics, and technology in general to the extreme. After living two months and a half in Japan, I can see why the birthrates are so low and the loneliness epidemic is at an all-time high. Waiters are replaced by kiosks, and the convenience stores feel more like an extension of a vending machine than a neighborhood New York bodega. You can easily go an entire day here traversing the city, buying meals, and navigating train stations without making eye contact or exchanging a single word with another human being. I have done it before when my friends had conflicting schedules and while it is efficient, it is completely sterile and lifeless. Oftentimes I would be looking over my shoulder or making comments when I know no one is around me, but as humans we long for connection. When every day and first-world problems are wiped away, the muscle memory for casual human interaction and small talk just atrophies and goes away with it. We isolate ourselves in these perfectly curated, frictionless digital bubbles because dealing with a machine is predictable, whereas interacting with a human is messy and carries the risk of rejection.
