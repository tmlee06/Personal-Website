The Transgender Athlete Dilemma   
Inclusion or fairness? Is it possible to have both in competitive sports? Under Title IX, the federal law prohibits sexual discrimination in federally funded educational programs. Still, in recent years, the inclusion of transgender athletes in competitive sports has ignited widespread public and political debate, particularly in the context of college women’s sports. Inclusion advocates push for social equality, acceptance, and the right to participate in sports, believing that with appropriate regulations and guidelines, transgender athletes can compete fairly. On the other hand, the advocates of biological sex emphasize the intrinsic biological differences between males and females, claiming that the involvement of transgender women in women's sports would compromise fairness, safety, and the integrity of women’s sports. They believe in keeping the categories separate based on the sex assigned at birth. This year, San Jose State University’s (SJSU) women's volleyball team became the center of controversy after one of the players was revealed to be a biological male. As a result, multiple teams forfeited matches against SJSU, and lawsuits were filed. Recently, a federal judge ruled to allow the player to continue playing in the league. Rachel Nostrant and Kate Selig from *The* *New York Times* sideline with the judge’s ruling in their joint article “Judge Allows San Jose State Transgender Volleyball Player to Compete.” However, Jackson Thompson, a *Fox News* sports writer, disagrees with the judge’s ruling, arguing that Title IX has been misused to harm female athletes instead of protecting them in his article “Women volleyball players feeling ‘unsafe’ over trans player lose appeal of Biden judge’s ‘shameful’ ruling.” Nostrant and Selig pair, and Thompson utilize Aristotelian appeals, Toulmin claims, and warrants to build two arguments rooted in differing levels of Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs.  
	Nostrant and Selig use credible sources and legal frameworks to establish claims of facts and ethos. The pair states, “Judge S. Kato Crews, an appointee of President Biden to the U.S. District Court in Colorado, wrote in his ruling that appellate and Supreme Court precedents had established that the protections of Title IX and the 14th Amendment applied to transgender individuals.” Crews’ ruling strengthens the argument by appealing to readers' trust in the legal system (Nostrant and Selig). Nostrant and Selig outline the rules and regulations governing transgender eligibility in NCAA and USA Volleyball, emphasizing their lengthy approval process (Nostrant and Selig). The pair assures readers that transgender athletes can only participate in college sports after meeting all requirements, dispelling the misconception that male athletes can casually change their gender identity to gain a competitive advantage in sports.   
	Nostrant and Selig apply logos by presenting factual evidence and logical reasoning to defend the inclusion of transgender athletes while minimizing emotional appeals and pathos. The pair briefly features a comment from Tom Temprano, an LGBTQ civil rights group spokesman, describing the Title IX complaint as “frivolous, hateful and divisive” but avoids furthering the progressive narratives on Crews’ ruling (Nostrant and Selig).  Nostrant and Selig persuade readers, stating that “the player has been a member of the team since the 2022 season” (Nostrant and Selig). The pair further investigates that “the four conference schools that forfeited games against San Jose State this season played the Spartans in previous seasons when the player was on the team.” Their evidence aligns with the federal judge’s ruling that the opposition “weakened their case by not acting sooner” (Nostrant and Selig). Nostrant and Selig also report the NCAA’s strict guidelines and regulations that transgender athletes must comply with to participate in college sports, such as the suppression of testosterone, an essential hormone to male physical characteristics (Nostrant and Selig). The pair continues to argue that “in some sports, like track and field, transgender women are essentially barred from competing in women’s events” (Nostrant and Selig). Acknowledging the opposing side’s warrant that male puberty before transitioning to women may give transgender athletes physical advantages over their biological female counterparts, the pair notes, “There is debate about the extent to which testosterone provides a decisive advantage in athletics” (Nostrant and Selig). Nostrant and Selig adopt a warrant that under the strict guidelines within the well-governed college sports framework, transgender athletes should be treated no differently than their female counterparts.  
	Thompson’s article employs ethos by incorporating detailed firsthand accounts of Brooke Slusser, SJSU women’s volleyball team co-captain and the transgender athlete's teammate. Slusser, along with others, filed lawsuits over the presence of her transgender teammate (Thompson). Despite the sensitivity and controversy surrounding the issue, Slusser's decision to speak publicly adds significant credibility to the narrative; she is not an outsider commenting from the sidelines but a direct participant who has lived through the challenges she describes. Her willingness to attach her name to her claims proves her authenticity.   
Thompson appeals to pathos by emphasizing the emotional toll Slusser endured as a teammate of a transgender player, tying this to claims of value and claims of policy. Slusser alleges “she was made to share living spaces and changing spaces with \[the transgender player\] while the university withheld \[the said player’s\] natural birth sex from Slusser and other players on the team” (Thompson). Thompson demonstrates how individual security needs are not fulfilled by offering readers realistic and relatable examples of transgender athletes in women’s sports. Thompson further argues that the discomfort and confusion that female players felt in those situations was not taken into account when SJSU repeatedly displayed “favoritism for \[the transgender player\] at the expense of the women on the team” (Thompson). He refers to the assistant coach’s suspension due to her filing a Title IX complaint over the transgender player's presence, which resulted in female players feeling unsupported and having no outlet to discuss this issue around them (Thompson). Slusser reflects on the assistant coach as someone “you could voice how you felt, and she could comfort you and validate your feelings and at least make you feel heard compared to the other coaches” (Thompson). Thompson's use of Slusser’s emotional struggles conveys the warrant that current policies prioritizing the inclusion of transgender athletes in women’s sports fail to consider the feelings of female athletes. He suggests that these policies are flawed and should instead prioritize the safety and well-being of female athletes.  
Thompson cites the comments from Bill Bock, a legal counsel of the Independent Council on Women’s Sports (ICONS), to provide an authoritative perspective as his pathos. Bock criticizes that the governing bodies of college sports, such as the NCAA and the Mountain West Conference, in which SJSU participates, are not “protecting fair competition and the safety of athletes” (Thompson).  Bock further insists that “the harm caused to numerous women and women’s terms by a trans-identifying male competing in Mountain West Conference women’s college volleyball stem from illegal Conference and NCAA rules which are resulting in substantial and continuing violations of federal laws” (Thompson). Thompson urges that the current system of transgender athletes participation in college sports has to be reevaluated by featuring Bock’s comments of female athletes “ultimately receiving justice” (Thompson). However, Thompson's article has no data on physical harm done to female athletes, leading readers to question his word choice of ‘unsafe’ in the title. Additionally, the fact that Thompson discloses the name of the transgender athlete in question, whereas Nostrant and Selig do not, further makes readers question his ethics as a journalist.

Through both articles, Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs provides a valuable lens for understanding why these opposing sides struggle to find common ground. Nostrant and Selig’s article advocates prioritizing social and inclusion needs to create a more accepting sports environment to achieve self-actualization. However, the inclusion advocates dismiss the opposing side’s safety and security needs, failing to answer how to confront the discomfort that biological females feel when they have to share intimate spaces with biological males. Thompson’s article illustrates how female college athletes are consumed by fighting for their safety and security needs instead of fulfilling their ego and self-actualization needs. For many young athletes, college sports are the pinnacle of their careers, built on years of dedication. Their frustration with current transgender athlete policies and concerns over fairness and safety in women’s sports intensify the debate, often reaching out to the public for empathy. However, the advocates of barring transgender athletes' participation in women’s sports lack scientific clarification as to what is deemed fair and safe for female athletes, which will aid the opposing side in understanding their perspectives.  

In conclusion, Nostrant, Selig, and Thompson strategically engage Aristotelian appeals and the Toulmin Method to create arguments that reflect opposite sides of the transgender athlete debate. Nostrant and Selig focus on equality and self-actualization needs based on Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs, integrating ethos through citing legal rulings and institutional policies to support the inclusion of transgender athletes. The pair minimized pathos and focused on logos and claims of facts about why transgender athletes should be included, trusting the readers’ logical understanding. On the other hand, Thompson emphasizes female athletes’ safety and security needs. He uses ethos through Slusser’s account to persuade readers about the inequality on the current SJSU women’s volleyball team. Thompson extensively applies claims of value and claims of policy to instill sympathy among readers by citing how current transgender athlete policies deny equal opportunities for female athletes. Both articles use warrants to justify their claims. By tying their arguments to different levels of Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs, the readers can understand the complexity and the disagreement in college sports over transgender athletes.
