College students have gone from pulling all-nighters to going all-in. With the rise of mobile betting apps, many college students are turning in their textbooks for sportsbooks. They can create a parlay on their phone for everything from football games to the election's outcome. Accessibility and promises of big wins cloud the line between entertainment and addiction. Gambling on college campuses runs an especially high risk of addiction and loss, leading some to question whether the excitement is worth the cost. Oliver Staley’s article, “An Explosion in Sports Betting Is Driving Gambling Addiction Among College Students,” dives into this growing crisis, revealing that the aggressive marketing of sports betting apps has made gambling more accessible than ever. He describes how features like real-time wagers make students bet impulsively, frequently leading to financial struggles, poor academic performance, and mental health issues. In "An Explosion in Sports Betting Is Driving Gambling Addiction Among College Students," Oliver Staley applies the Toulmin claims of facts, value, and policy, using several warrants and assumptions in arguing that mobile sports betting apps have dangerously normalized gambling among college students.

Statistics, odds, and probabilities can be overwhelming, but through data, Staley uses claims of fact to demonstrate the significant impact of mobile sports betting on college students. He reports, “One out of 10 college students is a pathological gambler, according to one meta-analysis conducted by professors at the University of Buffalo, far higher than the 2-5% of the U.S. general population estimated to have a gambling problem” (Staley). His bar charts further back this data to illustrate the 16.3 million average monthly users of popular betting apps, up 600% from 2021 to 2023 (Staley). The widespread availability of smartphones is contributing to this dramatic increase. Staley cites Dr. James Sherer, who declares, “You don’t have to go to a casino, you don’t have to go to a bank, you can do it at work, you can do it in the middle of the night” (Staley). Sherer emphasizes how mobile betting apps remove traditional barriers, which can promote impulsive behavior, enabling users to lose control of their betting habits. The ease of online gambling appeals to young adults, many of whom lack financial stability or self-discipline. Staley supports this with a National College Athletic Association survey recording, "Nearly 60% have bet on sports, and 4% do so daily. Almost 6% reported losing more than $500 in a single day" (Staley). These numbers display not just the frequency of gambling on college campuses but also its financial toll. Staley also reports that $305 million was spent on television advertisements for sports betting through November 2023, supporting Sherer's observation: "Even if you are trying to avoid it, you are going to be served up more options and opportunities to re-engage" (Staley). This huge advertising spending uncovers how betting companies and sportsbooks consistently bombard people, making it impossible to avoid gambling temptations.   

Staley bases his argument on claims of value by bringing forth the ethical and social issues regarding mobile sports betting among college students. Early on, he explains, “Gambling addiction is a disorder recognized by the American Psychiatric Association…It’s often correlated with other forms of addiction, as well as anxiety and depression, experts say, and problem gambler are at greater risk of suicide” (Staley). Staley’s explanation urges the need to confront a problem that jeopardizes not only academic achievement and financial stability but also the lives of young people. He states how observers are drawing parallels between the rapid spread of mobile sports betting and the opioid crisis. Staley quotes Diana Goode, who warns, “I think we're only really seeing the beginning of what’s going to happen, especially with our kids with problems.” She continues, “It's the 20-something male betting on sports… 40% of our calls are from that demographic or about that demographic. Because it’s not just these kids that are calling, it’s their parents” (Staley). Her words demonstrate how addiction to gambling affects young adults and even goes further, touching their families. Staley's use of Goode's perspective reinforces the need to address mobile sports betting as a societal issue rather than an individual problem. He emphasizes the lack of institutional support for gambling addiction. He references Jim Lange, who explains that pathological gambling "doesn't have a home" and “aren't as visible” as other disorders within campus mental health services (Staley). This discrepancy in care leaves vulnerable students struggling without adequate resources to address their addiction.   

Staley melds claims of policy to explore changes that allowed the rapid increase of sports wagering within the United States. He proclaims that the “modern age of sports gambling began in 2018, when the U.S. Supreme Court struck down a 1992 federal law that restricted sports betting to a handful of states and Native American tribal lands” (Staley). He explains how the overturned decision granted states control over sports betting, leading over 30 states to legalize it for tax revenue, and professional sports leagues, once opposed, now flood sportscasts with betting ads (Staley). Staley then refers to Dr. Timothy Fong, who proposes "a national response undergirded by federal policy" (Staley). This includes increased supervision of mobile betting operators, significant federal research funding for gambling addiction, and tighter control of advertising (Staley). Fong also says, "We should be studying what those ads are doing to those people's brains. They are supposed to be regulated, but no state is very aggressive about going after them" (Staley). Staley weaves Fong's perspective to illuminate how both federal and state regulations fall short. Staley relies on warrants and assumptions to link the claim support and show how mobile sports betting presents serious risks to college students. Staley adopts Fong's insights to tie back to his warrants and assumptions, examining the pros and cons of mobile sports betting and its impact on college students.
